# Set up DQX Studio for your organization

This page is for the person standing up DQX Studio for a team — a data governance manager or workspace admin. You'll make a handful of decisions once, and every author, reviewer, and steward who follows inherits them. Think of it as a rollout playbook: work through the steps in order, and by the end your team can start authoring rules against a shared set of standards.

<Admonition type="note" title="You'll need the Admin role">
Everything below lives under **Admin Settings** and is only available to people with the **Admin** role. If you can't see Admin Settings, ask an existing admin to grant it — or to walk through these steps with you.
</Admonition>

## Step 1 — Map roles to your teams

**The decision:** who can look, who can author, who can approve, and who can run checks.

DQX Studio has four roles, from least to most access:

- **Viewer** — read-only. Can see rules, tables, and results.
- **Rule Author** — can create and edit their own draft rules.
- **Rule Approver** — can approve anyone's drafts, not just their own.
- **Admin** — everything, including these settings.

There's also a separate **Runner** privilege that sits alongside the roles. It grants permission to trigger runs (evaluate checks against data). It's additive — you can give it to a Viewer or an Author without changing their role. Admins can always run.

Rather than naming individuals, you map your existing **workspace groups** to these roles. Assign the group your analysts already belong to as Viewers or Authors, your leads as Approvers, and a small trusted group as Admins. When someone joins or leaves a group, their access follows automatically.

## Step 2 — Decide your approval policy

**The decision:** should a second person sign off before a rule goes live?

By default, DQX Studio uses **four-eyes approval**: an author submits a draft, and someone else reviews and approves it before it becomes active. Authors can't approve their own work unless they're also an approver or admin. This keeps a check on what gets applied to your data and gives you an audit trail of who approved what.

We recommend keeping approval on for production. For a sandbox or a trusted development environment where speed matters more than sign-off, you can allow **auto-approval** so drafts go live without a second reviewer.

## Step 3 — Define your standards

**The decision:** what "good quality" means for your organization, expressed once so everyone reuses it.

This is where you set the shared vocabulary your authors will pick from:

- **Governed tags** — reusable labels such as `class.location` or `class.email`. When an author tags a rule, the app suggests which columns it applies to. Define the tags your organization cares about here so authors aren't inventing their own.
- **Quality dimensions** — the categories you report against: completeness, validity, accuracy, consistency, uniqueness, and timeliness. Decide which matter most for your data so scores roll up in a way your team understands.
- **Default pass thresholds** — how strict a check has to be to count as passing (for example, "at least 90% of rows must pass"). Set a sensible default so authors don't start from scratch; they can still override per table.

## Step 4 — Set retention and handle sensitive data

**The decision:** how long you keep run history, and — separately — how long you keep sampled rows that may contain sensitive data.

When a check fails, DQX Studio never changes your data. Instead it samples a few of the failing rows into a "quarantine" view so a steward can see what went wrong. Those sample rows can contain real, sensitive values, so they get their own retention setting, independent from ordinary run history.

You control two things on the Governance tab:

- **Runs and metrics retention** — how long score history and run records are kept for trend reporting.
- **Quarantined sample retention** — how long the sampled failing rows are kept before they're cleared.

Set the sample retention as short as your triage process allows if the data is sensitive, and keep run metrics longer so your quality trends stay meaningful over time.

<Admonition type="caution" title="Sensitive data lives in samples, not scores">
Trend scores and counts are just numbers — safe to keep. The quarantined sample rows are the part that may hold sensitive values. Tune that retention deliberately.
</Admonition>

See [Governance controls](/docs/studio/governance/governance-controls) for the full set of retention and data-handling options.

## Step 5 — Set the timezone and display options

**The decision:** how times and results appear for everyone.

On the General tab you set the **display timezone** so run times and schedules read correctly for your team — worth doing before people start reading run history. You can also enable a workspace-wide **Global Results tab**, which gives everyone a single place to see quality across all monitored tables at once, and choose whether results appear directly on rules.

## Step 6 — Know that everything is audited

Every change to a rule, role, schedule, or setting is recorded — who made it, when, and what it was before and after. You don't have to switch anything on; it's always on. If you ever need to answer "who changed this and why", the record is there.

See [Governance controls](/docs/studio/governance/governance-controls) for where to find the audit history.

## Now invite your team

Your organization is set up. Point people to the right starting place:

- **Authors** — send them to the [Quickstart](/docs/studio/start-here/quickstart) to create their first rule.
- **Stewards** — send them to [Measure quality health](/docs/studio/running/measure-quality-health) to read scores and trends and act on failing checks.
